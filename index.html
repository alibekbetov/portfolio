01Executive Biography

Alibek Nurmagambetov is a commercial strategy and business development professional with 7+ years of international experience spanning the United States, Kazakhstan, and Germany. His career combines large-scale operational leadership, government-level investment promotion, multi-location retail commercial management, and private-sector project analytics — a rare intersection of strategy, data, and execution.

He began in the United States, first managing a four-store retail portfolio at Bridgestone Americas worth approximately $5.8M in annual revenue, where he drove 15–18% year-on-year growth in Year 2 through service expansion, bundled promotions, and disciplined associate development. He then joined Amazon as an Area Operations Manager at the MGE1 fulfilment centre in Braselton, Georgia — one of the company's high-volume large-item FCs — leading 100+ associates and 5 Process Assistants across 12-hour outbound pick operations. In that role, he increased throughput by 15–18% and reduced cost per unit by 10–12% by redesigning zone assignments using individual performance data captured from handheld scanners. Four of his five Process Assistants were subsequently promoted.

Returning to Kazakhstan during the COVID-19 period, Alibek joined Kazakh Invest, the national investment promotion agency, as a Business Development Advisor. There, he contributed to the 36-Project Baseline — a government-led programme to protect $1.6 billion in active international investment projects at a time when global FDI had collapsed 42%. He served as the primary English-language interface for European and Western investors, and his analytical work supported capital flow tracking and sector gap analysis across renewables, ICT, and infrastructure.

He subsequently moved into the private sector as a Business Development Analyst at Astana Stroi Invest (DAL Group Holding), where he built investment cases, financial models, market research, and commercial proposals across a EUR 5M+ annual project pipeline. His COVID-era housing demand analysis — integrating household income, unemployment, mortgage rates, population data, construction permits, and apartment price history across three Kazakhstani cities — is formally cited in his recommendation letter as having helped management avoid significant losses by redirecting resources away from high-risk markets.

Alibek completed his Master of Science in Business Development at HSBA Hamburg School of Business Administration in July 2025, graduating with a thesis on strategic pricing dynamics in digital platforms. His research applied econometric methods including regression analysis, SARIMA modelling, and price elasticity estimation to real-world marketplace data. During his studies, he also contributed to applied consulting projects including sustainability reporting for PRME, strategic design for Nordzucker's Innovation Day, and positioning work for the StePs stewardship initiative.

He is fluent in English and Russian — both native/bilingual — with working-level German, and holds a BBA in Economics from Kennesaw State University (2016). He is currently based in Hamburg and targeting senior commercial analytics, business development, and strategy roles in the European market.

04Career Narrative

The commercial logic behind each career move — in my own words.

2013: Starting with Business Fundamentals (Georgia State University)

I arrived in the United States with an ambition to understand how commercial organisations actually work — not just in theory, but in practice. The Associate Degree in Business Administration gave me the foundation: accounting, economics, marketing, operations. I immediately looked for ways to apply it.

2014–2017: Learning to Sell and Lead People (Bridgestone Americas)

Joining Bridgestone as a Sales Manager was a deliberate choice: I wanted to understand commercial performance at the ground level — P&L ownership, customer relationships, team management. Managing four stores across southwest Atlanta forced me to develop simultaneously across sales, service operations, people management, and financial accountability. The 15–18% growth I delivered in Year 2 came from understanding what was actually holding the stores back: limited service capability, inconsistent associate behaviour, and under-exploited bundling opportunities. Fixing those three things drove the numbers.

2017–2018: Scale, Data, and Operations (Amazon)

Amazon was a different kind of challenge: not revenue growth, but throughput efficiency at scale. Managing 100+ people in a 600,000 sq ft fulfilment centre required me to think systematically. The zone redesign project came from noticing that we were assigning people by availability rather than capability. Once I started using the scanner performance data to match individuals to zones, throughput improved immediately. That experience permanently shaped the way I approach analytical problems: find the lever, test the hypothesis, measure the result.

2020–2022: National Strategy and Investment Promotion (Kazakh Invest)

The move to Kazakh Invest happened during COVID — one of the most difficult environments for international investment. My role was to help maintain momentum across a $1.6B pipeline of active projects when normal travel, meetings, and deal-making had stopped. Working at the intersection of government policy, commercial analytics, and international investor relations gave me a perspective I could not have gained in private sector roles alone: how capital flows are tracked, how investment risk is framed at a national level, and how government agencies communicate with private sector counterparts.

2022–2025: Building the Analytical Toolkit (Astana Stroi Invest / HSBA)

The Astana Stroi Invest role was where I developed my analytical depth most fully. Building investment cases from scratch — demand modelling, pricing analysis, scenario planning, financial metrics — across 40+ proposals and a EUR 5M+ annual pipeline gave me the structured, repeatable commercial methodology I now carry into every analysis. Concurrently, the HSBA MSc challenged me to add econometric rigour: regression modelling, SARIMA, price elasticity — methods that bridge business judgement and quantitative evidence. The thesis on Steam Marketplace pricing dynamics applied this rigour to a real, complex dataset with 80,000+ data points.

Where I Am Heading

My target is commercial analytics and business development roles at the intersection of strategy and data — roles where the ability to build a credible forecast, challenge an assumption, and translate findings into commercial decisions creates genuine value. Europe is my market. Hamburg is my base. The combination of my operational depth, analytical training, and multi-market international experience is, I believe, genuinely rare at this career stage.

10Leadership Philosophy

My approach to leadership is grounded in three principles: give people visibility into why their work matters, use data to have honest conversations, and develop the people around you before you need their promotions to prove you did.

The Amazon Evidence

At Amazon, I had 5 Process Assistants — the supervisory layer between me and 100+ floor associates. My job was not just to hit today's throughput numbers but to build a team that could hit tomorrow's. I gave each PA increasing responsibility over time: shift planning involvement, KPI review participation, data-driven coaching conversations. I advocated internally for their progression. Four of five were promoted — an 80% promotion rate that I believe reflects the consistency of the development approach rather than any single gesture.

The zone redesign project also illustrates something important about how I lead: I used scanner data to have factual conversations with associates about performance — not as a punitive tool, but as a coaching mechanism. Knowing that someone's pick accuracy improves significantly in the morning vs. the afternoon, or that they perform better in lower-density zones, allowed me to assign them to the work that suited them best. Associates responded positively to being placed in roles where they could succeed.

What Good Leadership Looks Like to Me

  • Data creates fairness. When performance conversations are grounded in facts — scanner data, revenue numbers, completion rates — they become easier to have and easier to accept. I use data not to monitor people but to help them improve.
  • Development is a long game. The four Process Assistant promotions didn't happen overnight. They happened because I invested in each individual's progression consistently, over the full duration of my time at Amazon.
  • Consensus enables speed. In cross-functional environments (DAL Group, Kazakh Invest), moving quickly requires that all parties trust the assumptions. I invest time upfront in aligning people around a common set of facts — which makes execution faster downstream.
  • Calm under pressure creates stability. Prof. Dr. Johns noted my "calm, persuasive communication style" and ability to "approach complex situations with preparation and composure." This is something I have cultivated deliberately, particularly in environments where uncertainty is high.

"Assessing the Impact of Price Variations on User Engagement and Acquisition in the Steam Marketplace — A Study of Strategic Pricing Dynamics in Digital Game Platforms"

Research Summary

The thesis examines pricing strategies on Steam — Valve's dominant PC gaming marketplace — and their effects on platform revenue, user engagement, and player acquisition. The central research question: how do price variations (discounts, regional pricing, bundling) affect user behaviour on a two-sided digital platform, and what strategic pricing implications can be derived for publishers?

Methodology

  • Dataset: 80,000+ Steam game data points covering pricing, review scores, estimated ownership, and sales history — with time-series extension
  • Econometric methods: OLS regression, SARIMA time-series modelling, panel regression, price elasticity estimation
  • Proxy variable design: Reviews.Total as acquisition proxy, Revenue.Estimated as revenue proxy — validated against available benchmarks
  • Regional pricing analysis: Third-degree price discrimination across geographic markets, with elasticity interpretation by region
  • Bundling strategy analysis: Bundle size distribution, discount efficiency modelling, second-degree price discrimination frameworks

Key Findings

  • Discount frequency significantly impacts short-term user acquisition but creates habituation effects — users delay purchase anticipating sales
  • Price elasticity varies materially by game genre: strategy/RPG games show inelastic demand; casual/indie titles are highly elastic
  • Regional price discrimination (e.g. lower prices in Eastern Europe / CIS) generates meaningful incremental acquisition with limited cannibalisation of premium markets
  • Bundle size has a non-linear relationship with engagement — larger bundles reduce per-title engagement but improve total platform time
  • SARIMA modelling identified cyclical seasonality in purchase behaviour aligned with platform-wide sale events (Summer Sale, Winter Sale)
